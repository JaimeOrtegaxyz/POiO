# Style Guide — Cooking Philosophy

> This file defines how dishes are conceived, built, and presented. It is not a recipe book — it is the grammar and vocabulary for creating recipes. Swap this file for a different cooking philosophy and the skill adapts.

Distilled from analysis of ~450 globally-sourced recipes across Latin, Asian, Middle Eastern, Mediterranean, Indian, North African, and fusion cuisines.

---

## Core Philosophy

- **Flavor-dense:** Every ingredient earns its place. No filler.
- **Weeknight-fast by default:** Most dishes under 30 minutes active time. Longer cooks are for batch prep or weekend projects.
- **Method-agnostic:** Oven, stovetop, slow cooker — whatever works. The method serves the dish, not the other way around.
- **Healthy without preaching:** Naturally balanced, never calorie-counted. Vegetables are structural, not an afterthought.
- **Batch-minded:** Think about tomorrow's lunch. Braised dishes improve with time.
- **Global without apology:** Cumin + lime is as natural as garlic + olive oil. Gochujang belongs in a bolognese if it tastes good there.

## Flavor Profile Families

Each family has a spice anchor, a default acid, and a natural assembly pattern. Mix families deliberately — anchor in one, accent with another.

### Latin / Mexican
- **Spice anchor:** Dried chiles (ancho, guajillo, chipotle) + cumin + Mexican oregano
- **Acid:** Lime, always lime
- **Aromatics:** White onion, garlic, cilantro, fresh chiles (jalapeño, serrano, habanero)
- **Finishing:** Crema, avocado, pickled red onion, tajin, fresh cilantro, charred corn
- **Natural assembly:** Tacos, bowls over rice, caldos with garnish bar, tortas

### Indian
- **Spice anchor:** Cumin + turmeric + ground coriander + chilli powder; garam masala as finishing spice
- **Acid:** Lemon, yoghurt (fermented acid)
- **Aromatics:** Garlic-ginger paste, onion (cooked until deeply golden), whole spices bloomed in oil (cardamom, cloves, mustard seeds, curry leaves)
- **Finishing:** Fresh cilantro, yoghurt drizzle, crispy shallots, fresh green chilli
- **Natural assembly:** Curry over rice, with naan/roti for scooping

### East / Southeast Asian
- **Spice anchor:** Garlic + ginger + soy sauce + sesame oil; extended with fish sauce, lemongrass, gochujang, five-spice
- **Acid:** Rice vinegar, lime, fish sauce (fermented acid)
- **Aromatics:** Ginger, garlic, spring onion, lemongrass, galangal
- **Finishing:** Toasted sesame seeds, spring onion, chilli oil, crushed peanuts, fresh herbs (Thai basil, cilantro, mint)
- **Natural assembly:** Bowls over rice or noodles, stir-fries, brothy soups, sticky glazed proteins

### Middle Eastern
- **Spice anchor:** Za'atar + sumac + cumin; baharat for warmth (cumin + cinnamon + allspice + black pepper + coriander)
- **Acid:** Lemon, pomegranate molasses
- **Aromatics:** Garlic, onion, tahini
- **Finishing:** Tahini drizzle, pomegranate seeds, toasted pine nuts, fresh parsley and mint, pickled turnip, dukkah
- **Natural assembly:** Flatbread wraps, grain bowls with yoghurt swoosh base, fatteh

### Mediterranean
- **Spice anchor:** Garlic + chilli flakes + olive oil; oregano, fennel seeds, rosemary
- **Acid:** Lemon (juice and zest), red wine vinegar, sherry vinegar
- **Aromatics:** Garlic, onion, celery, fennel, anchovies (for umami)
- **Finishing:** Fresh herbs (parsley, basil), shaved parmesan, lemon zest, olive oil drizzle, capers
- **Natural assembly:** Pasta, traybakes, one-pan with bread for mopping

### North African
- **Spice anchor:** Harissa + cumin + smoked paprika + coriander; cinnamon for warmth in tagines
- **Acid:** Lemon, preserved lemon
- **Aromatics:** Garlic, onion, ginger, dried fruits (apricots, dates) for sweet contrast
- **Finishing:** Fresh cilantro, toasted almonds, yoghurt, pomegranate seeds, olive oil
- **Natural assembly:** Tagines over couscous, grain bowls, flatbread wraps

### Fusion / Freestyle
- Anchor in ONE primary profile, accent with another
- Always have a reason for the cross-pollination (Korean-Mexican works because gochujang and chipotle share smoky-sweet-fermented DNA)
- Don't just mix randomly — find the flavor bridge between cuisines

## 7 Sauce Archetypes

These cover ~90% of all sauces encountered. Learn the formula, vary the specifics.

### 1. Tomato Base + Cream + One Bold Flavor
Sauté aromatics → add tomato (canned, paste, or fresh) → simmer → stir in cream element (cream, coconut milk, media crema, yoghurt) → add the bold flavor (harissa, chipotle, gochujang, nduja, miso). The bold flavor is what makes each version distinct.

### 2. Yoghurt + Acid + Herb
Yoghurt + lemon/lime juice + one fresh herb (mint, cilantro, dill) + pinch of salt. Optional: garlic, spice (cumin, sumac), tahini. This is the universal cooling counterpoint. Appears in 50%+ of dishes across all cuisines.

### 3. Sticky Asian Glaze
Soy sauce + honey/brown sugar + garlic + ginger, reduced until syrupy. Optional: gochujang, sriracha, oyster sauce. Thicken with cornstarch slurry if needed. Creates caramelized, lacquered finish on proteins.

### 4. Peanut-Soy Dressing
Peanut butter + soy sauce + lime juice + chilli (fresh or flakes) + water to thin. Optional: coconut milk for richness, fish sauce for funk, honey for sweetness. Works as dipping sauce, noodle dressing, or curry base.

### 5. Tahini Drizzle
Tahini + lemon juice + cold water, whisked until smooth and pourable. Season with salt, optional garlic. Savory, nutty, creamy — drizzle on everything from grain bowls to roasted veg to braised chicken.

### 6. Blended Green Sauce
Fresh herbs (cilantro, parsley, mint) + acid (lime, lemon, vinegar) + oil + optional heat (jalapeño, serrano). Blitz in a blender. Variants: salsa verde, aji verde, chimichurri, zhug, chermoula. Always raw, always bright.

### 7. Pan-Juice Deglaze
Sear protein → remove → deglaze pan with wine, vinegar, or stock → scrape up fond → reduce → finish with butter or cream. The fond (browned bits) IS the flavor. Don't waste it.

## 6 Assembly Patterns

### 1. The Bowl
**Formula:** Starch base + protein + 2 veg components (one cooked, one raw/pickled) + sauce/dressing + garnish + acid squeeze

This is the single most common format. The starch absorbs the sauce. The raw element provides crunch and brightness against the cooked protein. The garnish adds a final texture hit.

### 2. The Taco / Wrap
**Formula:** Warm tortilla/flatbread + protein with bold flavor + salsa or slaw (crunch + acid) + creamy element (crema, avocado, yoghurt) + fresh herb

Hand-held eating. The protein should be intensely seasoned because it shares space with strong supporting flavors. Always warm the tortilla.

### 3. The One-Pot
**Formula:** Sear protein → remove → sauté aromatics in the same pan → bloom spices → add liquid/sauce → return protein → add veg staged by cook time → finish with acid + herbs

Everything in one vessel. The liquid picks up the fond and spice. Vegetables go in based on how long they need — hardy ones first, delicate greens last.

### 4. The Traybake
**Formula:** Protein + hearty veg + sauce/paste on one tray → high heat oven → fresh elements added on top after cooking

Minimal effort, maximum caramelization. The oven does the work. Pair with a yoghurt sauce or fresh salad served alongside.

### 5. The Brothy
**Formula:** Build aromatic broth → simmer/poach protein in it → serve over starch → garnish bar

The broth IS the dish. It flavors the protein during cooking and becomes the serving liquid. Always offer a garnish bar: lime wedges, fresh herbs, chilli, avocado, tortilla strips or crispy shallots.

### 6. Batch-Then-Riff
**Formula:** Cook a large batch of one base (braised chicken, pulled chicken, slow cooker thighs) → serve 2-3 different ways across the week

Monday: over rice with the braising liquid as sauce. Tuesday: shredded into tacos with pickled onion and crema. Wednesday: tossed with pasta and a fresh green sauce. The base is the constant; the assembly changes everything.

## Technique Principles

### Texture Contrast Is Structural
Every dish needs at least two contrasting textures. This is not optional — it's what separates satisfying food from flat food.
- Crispy vs. creamy (charred skin + yoghurt swoosh)
- Crunchy vs. soft (pickled onion + braised thigh)
- Charred vs. fresh (blistered pepper + raw herb salad)
- Sources of crunch: toasted nuts/seeds, quick-pickled veg, crispy shallots, tortilla chips, panko crust, raw cabbage slaw

### Acid Finishes Everything
Acid is structural, not decorative. It balances richness, brightens flavor, and ties a dish together.
- Lime: Latin, Asian, Southeast Asian
- Lemon: Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, Indian
- Vinegar (rice, apple cider, sherry): dressings, quick pickles, deglazing
- Yoghurt/fermented elements count as acid too
- The squeeze of citrus at the end is never optional

### Bold Spice Layering
Don't add spices once and call it done. Layer them:
1. **In the marinade** (salt + spice penetrates the protein)
2. **Bloomed in oil** during cooking (toast whole spices or bloom ground spices in fat — 30 seconds to 2 minutes until fragrant)
3. **As a finishing element** (dukkah, za'atar, tajin, chilli flakes, sumac dusted on top)

### Fresh Herbs Are Non-Negotiable Garnish
- **Cilantro:** The workhorse. Latin, Asian, Indian, Middle Eastern — it goes with almost everything.
- **Parsley:** When cilantro would clash (Mediterranean, French-leaning dishes).
- **Mint:** Brightness in Asian, Middle Eastern, and Indian contexts. Pairs with yoghurt.
- **Spring onion:** Herb-adjacent. Sliced thin, always raw on top.
- Add herbs at the END, never cooked into the dish (exception: stems in stocks/broths).

### The Garlic-Ginger Backbone
Garlic and ginger appear together in 80% of savory dishes across all cuisines. When both are absent, herb pastes or spice blends take their place. They are the aromatic foundation — bloom them in oil before building the sauce.

### Vegetables Are Never "Just Sides"
Three roles for vegetables:
1. **Structural** — blended into sauces (roasted peppers into romesco, squash into curry base)
2. **Textural contrast** — raw slaw, quick-pickled onions, smashed cucumber, charred corn
3. **The star** — roasted cauliflower, charred cabbage, nopales, blistered peppers as the center of the plate alongside chicken

The "steamed veg on the side" approach does not exist here.

### Quick-Pickling
Thinly slice red onion (or radish, jalapeño, cucumber) → cover with acid (lime juice, vinegar) + pinch of salt + pinch of sugar → let sit 15-20 minutes. Instant brightness and crunch for any dish.

### Charring
Dry-fry vegetables in a hot pan or blister under the broiler. Corn, peppers, cabbage, spring onions, tomatoes — all improve with char. The slight bitterness adds depth that raw or steamed cannot.

### Rendering Fat First
If the recipe includes chicken skin, chorizo, or bacon — render the fat first in a cold pan, then use that fat to cook the aromatics. Free flavor, zero waste.

### Smashing for Surface Area
Press chicken thighs flat, smash potatoes, squash cucumbers — more surface area means more browning, more crunch, more flavor contact.

## 7 Marinade Archetypes

### 1. Yoghurt + Spice (Indian / Middle Eastern)
Yoghurt + spice blend (tandoori, harissa, baharat) + garlic + lemon. Tenderizes protein, promotes charring, carries spice into the meat. Minimum 30 minutes, ideally overnight.

### 2. Soy + Sweet + Ginger + Garlic (Asian)
Soy sauce + honey/brown sugar + grated ginger + garlic + sesame oil. Creates caramelized, lacquered finish. 20 minutes minimum.

### 3. Dried Chile Adobo (Mexican)
Rehydrate dried chiles (ancho, guajillo) in hot water → blend with vinegar, cumin, oregano, garlic. Deep red color, complex smoky heat. Minimum 1 hour, best overnight.

### 4. Dry Rub + Oil (Universal)
Mix dry spices with a little oil, rub onto protein. Simplest marinade. Best for high-heat cooking (oven roast, grill). Salt generously.

### 5. Citrus + Herb + Mustard (Mediterranean)
Lemon/lime juice + Dijon mustard + garlic + fresh herbs + olive oil. Bright, zingy, quick-acting. 20-30 minutes is enough.

### 6. Buttermilk / Acid Brine (American)
Buttermilk (or yoghurt thinned with milk) + salt + hot sauce or spices. Tenderizes deeply, seasons all the way through. Best for fried chicken. Minimum 4 hours, ideally overnight.

### 7. Miso + Mirin (Japanese Fusion)
White miso + mirin + a touch of oil. Creates deep umami caramelization when cooked. Excellent on chicken thighs under the broiler. 30 minutes minimum.

## Carb Pairing Logic

The starch is a flavor partner, not padding. Choose based on the sauce:

| Sauce Character | Best Starch | Why |
|----------------|-------------|-----|
| Thick, saucy, rich | Rice (jasmine or white) | Absorbs everything, neutral vehicle |
| Shredded, juicy protein | Tortillas, flatbread | Hand-held, scooping |
| Light, emulsified | Pasta | Tossed together, pasta water binds |
| Glazed, crispy protein | Roasted potatoes, fried rice | Matches texture, doesn't compete |
| Brothy, soupy | Rice or noodles IN the broth | Starch swims in flavor |
| Dry-rubbed, charred | Grain salad (couscous, bulgur, quinoa) | Textural complement, doesn't mask |

## Recipe Naming

Name dishes with flavor and personality, not generic descriptions:
- Lead with the distinctive element: "Chipotle-Honey Glazed Thighs" not "Chicken with Sauce"
- Include the texture or technique when it's the hook: "Smashed Crispy Chicken Bowl"
- Include the cuisine signal if fusion: "Korean-Style Sticky Chicken Tacos"
- Keep it punchy — 3-6 words
