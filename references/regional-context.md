## Seasonal Produce Calendar

### January – February (Cool Dry Season)
**Peak produce:** Broccoli, cauliflower, chayote, nopales, lettuce varieties, spinach, chard, radish, jicama, beets (betabel), cabbage, celery, peas
**Peak fruit:** Oranges, tangerines (mandarina), guava (guayaba), tejocote, strawberries (starting late Feb)
**Good for:** Hearty caldos, warm bowls, braised dishes, roasted veg traybakes, root vegetable sides. The cool weather calls for warming spices (canela, cloves, allspice) and rich broths.

### March – April (Warm Dry Season)
**Peak produce:** Nopales, spring onions, herbs becoming abundant, zucchini (starting), cucumber, radish, quelites (wild greens)
**Peak fruit:** Strawberries (peak), mango (starting late March), papaya
**Good for:** Transitional dishes — lighter soups, fresh salads starting to make sense, grilled preparations as weather warms. Mango salsa becomes viable.

### May – June (Pre-Rainy / Early Rains)
**Peak produce:** Zucchini (peak), squash blossoms (flor de calabaza), corn on the cob (elote season starts), chiles frescos abundant (all varieties), quelites, verdolagas
**Peak fruit:** Mango (peak), watermelon (sandía), cantaloupe (melón), papaya, pineapple
**Good for:** Elote-centric dishes, squash blossom preparations, bright salads with mango, charred corn bowls, fresh chile salsas. Peak season for bold, fresh flavors.

### July – August (Full Rainy Season)
**Peak produce:** Corn (peak), huitlacoche, squash blossoms (peak), chayote, tomatillo (peak), all chiles at peak, mushrooms (wild and cultivated), ejotes (green beans), calabaza
**Peak fruit:** Figs (higo), late stone fruits, tropical fruits continuing
**Good for:** Corn-forward dishes, seasonal specialties (huitlacoche quesadillas alongside chicken), rich sauces from fresh chiles, mushroom preparations. The rain makes produce abundant and cheap at tianguis.

### September – October (Late Rainy Season)
**Peak produce:** Pomegranate (granada — chiles en nogada season), mushrooms (wild), all squash varieties, sweet potato (camote), chayote, nopales
**Peak fruit:** Apple (manzana), pear (pera), guava (starting), persimmon (caqui), late figs, pomegranate
**Good for:** Harvest-style roasted preparations, walnut-based sauces (nogada), sweet potato as a side or in bowls, apple and pear in slaws or chutneys. Pomegranate seeds as garnish.

### November – December (Cool Dry Begins)
**Peak produce:** Jicama, beets, all cruciferous veg (broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts), celery root, chayote
**Peak fruit:** Guava (peak), tejocote, hawthorn, citrus starting (limes always, oranges and tangerines arriving), jamaica (dried hibiscus), tamarind
**Good for:** Warming stews, slow cooker dishes, roasted root vegetables, cabbage slaws, holiday-influenced flavors (canela, piloncillo, dried fruit). Great slow cooker season.

## Year-Round Staples in Guadalajara

These items are always available, cheap, and reliable:

**Produce:** White onion, garlic, tomato (jitomate), tomatillo, jalapeño, serrano, cilantro, lime, avocado, potatoes, carrots, chayote, nopales

**Dried chiles:** Ancho, guajillo, arbol, morita, chipotle, pasilla, cascabel, mulato — all widely available in bulk at mercados and tianguis

**Pantry:** Mexican oregano, canela (Ceylon cinnamon), piloncillo, achiote paste, corn tortillas (fresh daily), bolillo/telera, media crema, crema, queso fresco, queso Oaxaca, black beans (dried and canned), pinto beans

## Mexican Substitution Guide

When a recipe calls for something you can't find, these swaps work:

| Can't Find | Use Instead | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Sumac | Lime zest + tiny pinch citric acid | Captures the tartness |
| Tahini | Toasted sesame seeds blended with neutral oil | Process until smooth |
| Greek yoghurt | Media crema thinned with lime juice | Different tang, similar function |
| Fish sauce | Soy sauce + lime juice | Loses the funk but keeps the salt-acid |
| Gochujang | Chipotle in adobo + honey + soy sauce (blended) | Smoky-sweet-fermented is similar DNA |
| Coconut milk | Media crema (different but rich) | Works in curries, less tropical |
| Panko breadcrumbs | Bolillo/telera dried and crumbled, or blitzed tortilla chips | Bolillo gives great texture |
| Buttermilk | Milk + lime juice (let sit 10 min) | Or yoghurt thinned with milk |
| Mirin | Rice vinegar + sugar (2:1) | Approximate but functional |
| Nduja | Chipotle in adobo blended smooth | Same spicy-fatty-spreadable concept |
| Harissa | Blend: roasted red pepper + chipotle + cumin + garlic + olive oil | Captures the smoky chile heat |
| Crème fraîche | Media crema or crema mexicana | Nearly identical |

## Shopping Tips for Guadalajara

### Tianguis (Weekly Street Markets)
Best for: Seasonal produce, fresh herbs (cilantro, epazote, hierbabuena), dried chiles in bulk, dried beans, fresh tortillas, nopales, quelites
- Freshest and cheapest option
- Quality varies by vendor — find your regulars
- Peak selection during rainy season (July-September)

### Mercados (Permanent Markets — Mercado de Abastos, Mercado Libertad / San Juan de Dios)
Best for: Wide variety, bulk dried chiles, bulk spices, specialty Mexican ingredients, dried herbs, achiote, piloncillo
- Mercado de Abastos for wholesale prices on produce and staples
- Mercado Libertad for variety and specialty items

### Supermarkets (Soriana, Chedraui, La Comer, Walmart)
Best for: Convenience, imported ingredients, consistent stock, Asian aisle items
- La Comer tends to have the best specialty/imported section
- Soriana for everyday staples
- Walmart for budget

### Costco
Best for: Bulk spices, tahini, quality chicken, peanut butter, olive oil, coconut oil, sometimes frozen udon and specialty items
- Best price on tahini and sesame oil
- Quality meat selection (chicken thighs, whole birds)

### Specialty / Asian Stores
- Growing presence in GDL — search for Korean and Japanese stores for gochujang, miso, fish sauce, oyster sauce, rice noodles
- Some health food stores carry specialty items (nutritional yeast, specialty grains)

## Local Flavor Notes

Things to keep in mind when adapting globally-inspired recipes for a Guadalajara kitchen:

- **Limes are king.** You always have limes. Use them where recipes call for lemon — the flavor is slightly different but almost always works and often improves the dish in a Latin context.
- **Dried chiles are your superpower.** A Jalisco kitchen has access to more dried chile variety than most places in the world. Use this advantage — ancho for sweetness and depth, guajillo for bright heat, arbol for sharp fire, morita/chipotle for smoke.
- **Media crema is your secret weapon.** It substitutes for yoghurt, crème fraîche, heavy cream, and sour cream in various contexts. Keep it stocked.
- **Fresh tortillas change everything.** A recipe that seems simple becomes special with fresh corn tortillas from the tortillería. Never underestimate this.
- **Avocado is cheap here.** Use it generously where other regions might use it sparingly. Sliced, mashed, or as a creamy element — it's your local advantage.
- **Queso fresco and cotija** provide the salty-crumbly element that feta plays in Mediterranean cooking. Use them interchangeably where recipes call for feta.
